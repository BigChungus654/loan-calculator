How Credit Card Interest Works

Credit card interest is one of the most expensive forms of debt. Understanding how it works can motivate you to pay it off faster and save thousands of dollars.

The Minimum Payment Trap

Credit card companies set minimum payments low — usually 1-3% of your balance or a fixed amount like $25. While this seems helpful, it means you're mostly paying interest each month, barely touching the principal. A $5,000 balance at 22.99% APR with $100 minimum payments would take over 9 years to pay off and cost over $6,000 in interest.

How Extra Payments Help

Even small extra payments dramatically reduce your payoff time and total interest. Adding just $50 extra per month to the example above could cut your payoff time in half and save you thousands in interest.

Strategies to Pay Off Credit Card Debt

  • Avalanche Method: Pay minimums on all cards, put extra money toward the highest-interest card first. Saves the most money.
  • Snowball Method: Pay off the smallest balance first for psychological wins, then roll that payment into the next card.
  • Balance Transfer: Transfer to a 0% APR card to stop interest while you pay it down. Watch for transfer fees (usually 3-5%).
  • Debt Consolidation: Take out a lower-interest personal loan to pay off high-interest cards.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why does it take so long to pay off a credit card with minimum payments?

Because most of your minimum payment goes toward interest, not principal. At 22.99% APR, a $5,000 balance accrues about $96 in interest per month. If your minimum is $100, only $4 goes toward the actual debt.

How is credit card interest calculated?

Credit card interest is typically calculated daily using your APR divided by 365. This daily rate is applied to your average daily balance, then charged monthly. This means interest compounds, making it more expensive over time.

Should I pay more than the minimum?

Absolutely. Paying even $50-100 extra per month can save you years of payments and thousands in interest. Use this calculator to see exactly how much you'd save with different extra payment amounts.

What's a good strategy if I have multiple credit cards?

Pay minimums on all cards, then put any extra money toward either the highest-interest card (avalanche method — saves the most money) or the smallest balance (snowball method — gives you quick wins). Both work; choose what keeps you motivated.
