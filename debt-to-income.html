What This Means for You

Understanding Debt-to-Income Ratio

Your debt-to-income (DTI) ratio is one of the most important numbers lenders look at when you apply for a mortgage or loan. It measures how much of your monthly income goes toward debt payments.

How DTI Is Calculated

DTI = (Total Monthly Debt Payments ÷ Gross Monthly Income) × 100

DTI Ranges and What They Mean

  • 35% or less: Excellent. You're managing debt well and will qualify for the best loan terms.
  • 36% - 43%: Acceptable. Most conventional mortgages require a DTI under 43%. You can likely qualify but may not get the best rates.
  • 44% - 50%: Risky. You may still qualify for some loans (FHA allows up to 50% in some cases), but lenders see you as higher risk.
  • Over 50%: Too high. Most lenders will not approve a mortgage at this level. Focus on paying down debt first.

How to Lower Your DTI

  • Pay off credit cards and small debts
  • Avoid taking on new debt before applying for a mortgage
  • Increase your income through raises, side jobs, or adding a co-borrower
  • Refinance existing loans to lower monthly payments
  • Pay down car loans or student loans if possible

Frequently Asked Questions

What DTI do I need for a mortgage?

Most conventional loans require a DTI of 43% or less. FHA loans may allow up to 50% with compensating factors. VA loans are more flexible but generally want to see 41% or below. The lower your DTI, the better your chances of approval and the better your interest rate.

What debts are included in DTI?

DTI includes: mortgage/rent, car loans, student loans, credit card minimum payments, personal loans, child support, and alimony. It does NOT include: utilities, groceries, insurance (unless bundled with mortgage), subscriptions, or daily living expenses.

Front-end vs. back-end DTI — what's the difference?

Front-end DTI (or housing ratio) only includes housing costs. Back-end DTI includes all debts. Lenders typically want front-end DTI under 28% and back-end DTI under 36-43%. This calculator shows your back-end DTI.

Does my DTI affect my credit score?

DTI itself doesn't appear on your credit report and doesn't directly affect your credit score. However, high credit card balances (which contribute to DTI) do affect your credit utilization ratio, which impacts your score.
