Understanding Compound Interest

Albert Einstein reportedly called compound interest "the eighth wonder of the world." Whether or not he actually said it, the math behind compound interest is truly powerful — it's how ordinary people build extraordinary wealth.

The Compound Interest Formula

A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt)

Where:

  • A = Final amount
  • P = Principal (initial investment)
  • r = Annual interest rate (decimal)
  • n = Number of times interest compounds per year
  • t = Number of years

Simple vs. Compound Interest

Simple interest only earns interest on the original principal. Compound interest earns interest on the principal AND on previously earned interest. Over time, this creates exponential growth. For example, $10,000 at 7% simple interest earns $700/year forever. With compound interest, it earns $700 the first year, $749 the second, $801.43 the third, and so on — accelerating each year.

The Rule of 72

Want a quick estimate of how long it takes to double your money? Divide 72 by your interest rate. At 7%, your money doubles in about 10.3 years. At 10%, it doubles in about 7.2 years.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does compounding frequency matter?

Yes, but less than you might think. More frequent compounding gives slightly higher returns. For example, $10,000 at 7% for 20 years: annual compounding gives $38,697, while daily compounding gives $40,552. The rate and time period matter much more than compounding frequency.

What's a realistic rate of return?

The S&P 500 has historically returned about 10% annually before inflation (about 7% after inflation). High-yield savings accounts offer 4-5%. Bonds typically return 3-5%. Use a conservative estimate for planning — 7% is commonly used for long-term stock market investments.

Why is starting early so important?

Because of compound interest, time is more powerful than the amount you invest. $200/month from age 25 to 65 at 7% gives you about $528,000. Starting at 35 with $400/month at the same rate gives about $489,000 — less money despite contributing twice as much monthly.

How does inflation affect compound interest?

Inflation reduces the purchasing power of your returns. If you earn 7% but inflation is 3%, your real return is about 4%. This calculator shows nominal returns. For inflation-adjusted estimates, subtract the expected inflation rate (typically 2-3%) from your interest rate.
