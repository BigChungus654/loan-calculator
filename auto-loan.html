How Auto Loans Work

An auto loan is a secured loan used to purchase a vehicle. The car itself serves as collateral, which typically means lower interest rates compared to unsecured loans. Understanding how auto loans work helps you negotiate better terms and save money.

Key Factors That Affect Your Car Payment

  • Vehicle Price: The sticker price or negotiated price of the car. Always try to negotiate below MSRP.
  • Down Payment: Money you pay upfront. A larger down payment means a smaller loan and lower monthly payments.
  • Trade-In Value: The value of your current vehicle applied toward the new purchase, reducing the amount you need to finance.
  • Sales Tax: Most states charge sales tax on vehicle purchases, typically 5-10% of the purchase price.
  • Interest Rate (APR): The annual cost of borrowing. Rates depend on your credit score, loan term, and whether the car is new or used.
  • Loan Term: How long you have to repay. Shorter terms mean higher payments but less total interest paid.

New vs. Used Car Loan Rates

New car loans typically have lower interest rates (5-7% average) compared to used car loans (7-10% average). However, new cars depreciate faster. Consider the total cost of ownership, not just the monthly payment.

Frequently Asked Questions

What credit score do I need for an auto loan?

You can get an auto loan with most credit scores, but rates vary significantly. Excellent credit (720+) gets rates around 4-6%. Good credit (660-719) gets 6-9%. Fair credit (600-659) gets 9-14%. Poor credit (below 600) may see rates of 14-20% or higher.

Should I choose a longer loan term for lower payments?

While a 72 or 84-month loan lowers your monthly payment, you'll pay significantly more in interest and risk owing more than the car is worth (negative equity). Financial experts recommend keeping auto loans at 60 months or less.

Is it better to finance through a dealer or bank?

It's best to get pre-approved by your bank or credit union first, then compare with dealer financing. Sometimes dealers offer promotional rates (like 0% APR), but these often require excellent credit and may mean you can't negotiate on price.

How much car can I afford?

A common rule of thumb is that your total car expenses (payment, insurance, gas, maintenance) should not exceed 15-20% of your take-home pay. Your monthly car payment alone should ideally be under 10% of your gross monthly income.

Should I pay off my auto loan early?

If your loan doesn't have prepayment penalties, paying it off early saves you interest. Even small extra payments toward principal can reduce your total interest cost. Check your loan agreement for any prepayment penalty clauses.
